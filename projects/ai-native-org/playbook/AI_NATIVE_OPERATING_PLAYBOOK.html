Core Principles

These principles guide every decision in the AI-native transformation. When in doubt, return to these.

1. Humans for Judgment, AI for Execution
AI handles volume work and pattern work. Humans focus on judgment, creativity, and accountability. Every role should maximize time spent on high-judgment activities.
2. Architect, Don't Execute
AI-native roles design systems that execute at scale. The measure of success is system output, not personal output. 10x leverage comes from building, not doing.
3. Capability Over Headcount
Measure capacity in capability units (human + AI), not headcount. Growth comes from expanding capability, which may or may not require more people.
4. Customer Experience First
Every AI implementation must improve customer experience. Internal efficiency gains that degrade customer experience are not acceptable trade-offs.
5. Learn Fast, Fail Small
AI capabilities evolve rapidly. Design for experimentation. Small failures that generate learning are more valuable than slow, "safe" rollouts.
6. Transparency and Accountability
AI systems must be explainable. Human accountability remains even when AI executes. Customers deserve to know when they're interacting with AI.

AI-Augmented Engineer

Role Summary

AI-Augmented Engineers design software systems, specify requirements for AI code generation, review and refine AI output, and handle complex edge cases that require human judgment. They leverage AI to achieve 1.5-2x productivity while maintaining quality standards.

Core Responsibilities

  • System Design: Architecture decisions, API contracts, data models
  • Specification: Writing detailed prompts/specs for AI code generation
  • Review & Refinement: Evaluating AI-generated code for correctness, security, performance
  • Edge Case Handling: Implementing complex logic AI struggles with
  • AI Tool Optimization: Improving prompts, training custom models on codebase patterns

Competency Model

Competency Junior (L1-L2) Mid (L3-L4) Senior (L5+)
AI Tool Proficiency Uses Copilot effectively for autocomplete Crafts complex prompts, uses multiple AI tools strategically Customizes AI tools, trains on codebase, builds internal tooling
Code Review Reviews AI output for obvious errors Catches subtle bugs, security issues, performance problems Defines review standards, mentors team on AI output evaluation
System Design Implements designs specified by others Designs subsystems, considers AI execution constraints Architects systems optimized for AI + human collaboration
Specification Writing Writes basic prompts for simple features Writes comprehensive specs that AI can implement correctly Defines specification standards, creates templates for team

Time Allocation

Activity Traditional Engineer AI-Augmented Engineer
Writing code from scratch 60% 15%
Reviewing/refining AI-generated code 0% 30%
System design & architecture 15% 25%
Writing specifications/prompts 5% 15%
Debugging & troubleshooting 15% 10%
Meetings & collaboration 5% 5%

Hiring Profile

What to Look For
  • Strong fundamentals (algorithms, data structures, system design) - AI amplifies good judgment
  • Experience with AI coding tools (Copilot, Cursor, Claude) and ability to articulate what works
  • Critical evaluation skills - can quickly identify when AI output is wrong or suboptimal
  • Communication skills - can write clear specifications that both humans and AI understand
  • Learning velocity - AI tools evolve rapidly, need to stay current
Red Flags
  • Over-reliance on AI without critical evaluation ("AI wrote it, must be right")
  • Resistance to AI tools ("I prefer to write everything myself")
  • Weak fundamentals hidden by AI assistance
  • Unable to explain AI-generated code they're submitting

Product Architect

Role Summary

Product Architects define product outcomes, design systems for achieving those outcomes, and train AI systems on user patterns. They shift from "what features to build" to "what systems will achieve user outcomes at scale."

Core Responsibilities

  • Outcome Definition: Translating business goals into measurable user outcomes
  • System Design: Designing product systems (not just features) that achieve outcomes
  • AI Training: Training AI systems on user patterns, edge cases, and judgment calls
  • Experiment Design: Setting up experiments to learn what AI-driven interventions work
  • Cross-Pod Coordination: Ensuring capabilities compose into coherent user experiences

Key Transformation: From Features to Systems

Traditional PM Product Architect
Writes PRD for "Add export to CSV feature" Designs "Data Portability System" that uses AI to identify what users want to export and offers appropriate formats
Prioritizes feature backlog based on customer requests Designs AI system that identifies customer needs from usage patterns and generates personalized recommendations
Runs quarterly roadmap planning Designs continuous experiment system that automatically tests and rolls out improvements
Reviews customer support tickets for product insights Trains AI to analyze all customer interactions and surface patterns requiring product attention

Growth Engineer

Role Summary

Growth Engineers build automated systems for customer acquisition, activation, and expansion. They replace manual sales/marketing motions with AI-driven growth loops.

Core Responsibilities

  • Acquisition Systems: Building AI-powered content generation, SEO automation, paid campaign optimization
  • Activation Systems: Designing AI-driven onboarding flows that personalize based on user signals
  • Expansion Systems: Building AI that identifies expansion opportunities and orchestrates outreach
  • Attribution & Measurement: Creating systems that track ROI across AI-driven touchpoints
  • High-Value Exceptions: Handling leads/accounts that require human touch

Key Metrics Owned

Metric Target How AI Helps
Cost per Acquisition (CPA) 50-70% reduction vs. sales-led AI content generation, automated outreach, campaign optimization
Time to Value (TTV) <24 hours for core value AI onboarding that adapts to user behavior
Product Qualified Lead (PQL) Rate >30% of signups AI identifies high-intent users from behavior patterns
Expansion Revenue % >30% of total revenue AI identifies expansion signals, automates upgrade prompts

Support Architect

Role Summary

Support Architects design and operate AI systems that handle customer support at scale. They shift from resolving individual tickets to building systems that resolve categories of issues automatically.

Core Responsibilities

  • AI Resolution System Design: Building AI agents that handle common support scenarios
  • Knowledge Management: Creating and maintaining knowledge bases that AI draws from
  • Escalation Design: Defining when and how issues escalate to humans
  • Quality Monitoring: Evaluating AI responses for accuracy and customer satisfaction
  • High-Judgment Escalations: Handling complex, sensitive, or high-stakes customer issues

Resolution Tier Model

Tier Handler Issue Types Target %
Tier 0 Self-serve (docs, in-app) How-to questions, common issues with documented solutions 40%
Tier 1 AI Agent Account issues, troubleshooting, configuration help 35%
Tier 2 Support Architect Complex technical issues, billing disputes, feature requests 20%
Tier 3 Specialist/Engineering Bugs, security issues, enterprise escalations 5%

Key Metrics

  • AI Resolution Rate: % of tickets resolved without human intervention (target: >60%)
  • Customer Satisfaction (AI): CSAT for AI-handled tickets (target: >4.0/5.0)
  • Escalation Rate: % of AI interactions requiring human escalation (target: <15%)
  • Human Intervention Rate: % of AI responses requiring human correction (target: <5%)

Analytics Architect

Role Summary

Analytics Architects define the questions worth answering, design systems for AI-powered analysis, and synthesize insights that require human judgment. They shift from running queries to architecting insight systems.

Core Responsibilities

  • Analysis System Design: Building AI systems that answer questions at scale
  • Quality Validation: Evaluating AI analysis for accuracy and relevance
  • Insight Synthesis: Combining AI outputs into actionable recommendations
  • Stakeholder Communication: Translating data into decisions for non-technical audiences

AI Operations Manager

Role Summary

AI Operations Managers orchestrate the deployment, monitoring, and optimization of AI systems across the organization. They ensure AI agents operate reliably and that human oversight is maintained.

Core Responsibilities

  • AI Agent Deployment: Rolling out AI systems with appropriate guardrails
  • Performance Monitoring: Tracking AI system health, accuracy, and efficiency
  • Cost Optimization: Managing AI compute costs, optimizing for ROI
  • Incident Response: Handling AI system failures and edge cases
  • Governance: Ensuring AI systems comply with policies and regulations

Key Dashboards Owned

  • AI System Health: Uptime, latency, error rates for all AI agents
  • Human Intervention Tracker: When and why humans override AI decisions
  • Cost Center: AI compute costs by system, trending, ROI
  • Quality Scores: Accuracy metrics across AI systems
  • Compliance Status: AI governance policy adherence

Part II: Phase 0 - Foundation (Months 1-3)

Phase 0 establishes the assessment baseline, designs the target operating model, and aligns leadership. This phase is critical—skipping it leads to failed transformations.

Objectives

  • Establish baseline AI maturity across all functions
  • Design target operating model (org structure, roles, metrics)
  • Achieve executive alignment on transformation scope and investment
  • Identify pilot candidates for Phase 1
  • Create change management and communication strategy

Workstreams

Workstream 1: AI Maturity Assessment

1
Current State Inventory
Document all AI tools currently in use, who uses them, for what purposes, and with what results. Include shadow AI (personal ChatGPT usage, etc.).
2
Workflow Analysis
Map key workflows in each function. Identify which steps are high-volume/pattern-based (AI candidates) vs. high-judgment (human focus).
3
Data Infrastructure Assessment
Evaluate data quality, accessibility, and governance. AI systems require clean, accessible data to function effectively.
4
Cultural Readiness Survey
Assess organizational sentiment toward AI: enthusiasm, fear, skepticism. Identify champions and resistors.

Workstream 2: Target Operating Model Design

1
Future State Org Design
Design capability pod structure. Define which pods exist, what capabilities each delivers, and how they compose.
2
Role Evolution Mapping
Map every current role to its AI-native evolution. Identify skill gaps and training needs.
3
Metrics Framework Design
Define new metrics for productivity, quality, and business impact. Design measurement systems.
4
Technology Stack Definition
Select AI platforms, tools, and infrastructure. Make build vs. buy decisions.

Headcount Implications

Critical: Handle with Care

AI-native transformation changes headcount needs. This must be handled with transparency and humanity:

  • Upskilling First: Invest heavily in helping current employees transition to new roles
  • Attrition Absorption: Use natural attrition to reduce headcount where appropriate
  • Redeployment: Move people to higher-value work before considering separations
  • Generous Transitions: When separations are necessary, provide generous severance and support

Part IV: Change Strategy

AI-native transformation fails more often from change management failure than from technology failure. This section provides the human side of the equation.

Change Management Principles

  • Transparency Over Comfort: Tell people what's changing and why, even when it's uncomfortable
  • Investment Over Abandonment: Invest in helping people transition, don't just replace them
  • Evidence Over Assertion: Show results from pilots before demanding broader adoption
  • Inclusion Over Imposition: Involve people in designing their new roles
  • Speed Over Perfection: Move quickly, learn from mistakes, iterate

Stakeholder Map

Stakeholder Group Primary Concern Key Message
Executives Business results, risk "This is how we compete in the next decade"
Board ROI, governance "Structured investment with clear milestones"
Managers Team disruption, their role "Your role evolves to AI orchestration, not elimination"
Individual Contributors Job security, skill relevance "We're investing in your transition to higher-value work"
Customers Service quality, data privacy "Better, faster service with human oversight"

Managing Resistance

Common Resistance Patterns

Pattern What You'll Hear Response Strategy
Fear "Am I going to lose my job?" Acknowledge fear. Be honest about changes. Commit to transition support.
Skepticism "AI isn't ready for real work" Show evidence from pilots. Let skeptics evaluate firsthand.
Identity "I'm a software engineer, not a prompt writer" Reframe role evolution as growth. Celebrate new skills.
Control "I don't trust AI to do this right" Design systems with human oversight. Build trust gradually.
Inertia "We've always done it this way" Create urgency around competitive threat. Show what's possible.

Intervention Framework

1
Listen First
Understand the specific concern. Don't dismiss or argue immediately.
2
Acknowledge Validity
Most concerns have some validity. Acknowledge what's true.
3
Provide Context
Share the bigger picture: why this matters, what alternatives exist.
4
Offer Agency
Give people choices and involvement in how changes affect them.
5
Follow Up
Check back. Adjust based on feedback. Show you heard them.
