Strategic Solutions Operating Model

Stakeholder Summary • December 2025 • For Review

Purpose of This Document

This week I had the opportunity to speak with several of you about Commerce.com's strategic direction, technical challenges, and organizational structure. This summary synthesizes those conversations into a proposed operating model for how we might approach complex, cross-platform customer solutions.

I'm sharing this to get your feedback on the feasibility, identify gaps in my understanding, and validate whether the assumptions I'm making align with your on-the-ground reality.

The Core Problem We're Solving

Based on our conversations, I've identified a pattern that shows up across customer migrations, integrations, and complex implementations:

The "Jig Building" Problem

What I Heard: When customers need complex solutions—like migrating from WooCommerce, integrating BC + Feedonomics + Makeswift into a unified workflow, or building custom agency tooling—we often build one-off "jigs" (specialized tooling and processes) that work but never scale beyond the first customer.

The Pattern: Solutions Architects solve the problem brilliantly for Customer 1, but the solution stays on their laptop. When Customer 2 arrives with the same need, we start from scratch. The knowledge doesn't transfer, the tooling doesn't graduate to Core Platform, and we're perpetually in "artisan mode" instead of "manufacturing mode."

The Opportunity: What if we formalized the "jig building" process? Build the solution once with proper instrumentation, validate unit economics with 10 pilot customers, document the playbook, then transfer it to Core Platform as a repeatable capability.

The Proposed Operating Model

What This Role Does

I lead a small, temporary team (3-5 people) that takes on high-value, high-complexity customer scenarios that don't fit cleanly into existing product roadmaps. Think of it as an internal R&D function for market solutions:

  • Identify: Work with Sales/Product to spot patterns—"We've lost 3 deals this quarter because we can't migrate WooCommerce stores"
  • Build the Jig: Assemble a temporary Tiger Team (leased engineers from BC/Feedonomics/Makeswift) to build the integration tooling
  • Validate: Deploy to first 10 customers, measure time-to-first-value, NPS, and unit economics
  • Transfer: Hand the validated solution to Core Product teams, disband the Tiger Team, move to the next problem

How This Works in Practice

The model relies on three tactical mechanisms:

Mechanism What It Does Why It Matters
Resource Leasing I negotiate time-bound "loans" of engineers from your teams (e.g., "I need 1 Catalyst engineer for 8 weeks") Avoids hiring a standing team. Engineers return to you after the project completes.
Interface Contracts I define the "what" (API contract, data schema), you own the "how" (implementation) Clear boundaries. I don't tell you how to build your platform—I tell you what the integration needs.
Unblocker Syncs Weekly 30-min decision-making meeting (no status reports, only blockers) Keeps distributed teams coordinated without adding bureaucracy.

Example: WooCommerce Migration (Instance 001)

To make this concrete, here's how the model would work for the WooCommerce opportunity:

6-Month Pilot Timeline

Month 1-2 (Reality Check): Manually migrate 1 WooCommerce store with a Solutions Architect to identify technical blockers and gap analysis.

Month 3-4 (Build the Jig): Tiger Team builds migration tooling (data transformation pipelines, schema mapping, cutover automation). Not production-grade yet—"good enough" to prove the concept.

Month 5-6 (First 10 Alpha): Deploy to 10 pilot customers (likely via WP Engine partnership). Measure: Time to first value, NPS, technical issues, support burden.

Month 7+ (Scale or Kill): If unit economics validate (TTFV <3 weeks, NPS >40), transfer to Core Platform. If not, kill the initiative or pivot the approach.

What I Need From Your Teams

This model only works if you're willing to experiment with resource lending. For the WooCommerce pilot, here's what I'd request:

  • From Marcus/Anthony (Engineering): 1-2 engineers (Backend + Frontend) for 8-12 weeks to build data transformation pipelines and migration UI
  • From Jordan (Product): Alignment on prioritization—this can't conflict with critical roadmap commitments, but needs protected capacity
  • From Alan (Makeswift): Technical guidance on storefront migration patterns—likely not direct engineering time unless Makeswift integration is in scope

In exchange, I commit to:

  • Returning your engineers on schedule (or sooner if we kill the initiative)
  • Transferring all IP, code, and playbooks to Core Platform teams
  • Not creating technical debt that becomes "your problem" to maintain

What I'm Testing With This Hypothesis

I'm not assuming this model will work as-is. I need to validate several assumptions during my first 30 days:

Assumption 1: Resource Flexibility

Can VPs/Directors actually loan engineers for 8-12 week sprints, or are roadmaps too locked to allow this?

Assumption 2: Cross-Platform Appetite

Do BigCommerce, Feedonomics, and Makeswift teams see value in cross-platform solutions, or do vertical product priorities dominate?

Assumption 3: "Good Enough" Tolerance

Can we ship a "works but not perfect" v1 to validate demand, or does everything need production-grade quality on Day 1?

Assumption 4: Transfer Mechanism

After First 10 validation, do Core Platform teams have capacity to absorb ownership, or will Tiger Team solutions become orphaned?

What I Need From You

This document is a hypothesis, not a mandate. I'm sharing it with you to:

  1. Validate Ground Truth: Tell me where my assumptions are wrong. If resource lending is a non-starter, I need to know now.
  2. Identify Friction Points: What blockers do you foresee? Budget? Headcount? Political resistance? Technical constraints?
  3. Pressure-Test the Model: Have you seen this pattern work (or fail) elsewhere? What am I missing?
  4. Understand Your Constraints: What would make this easy for you to support? What would make it impossible?

Next Steps

I'm proposing a 30-day validation period where I test these assumptions without committing the organization to a full operating model:

Week 1-2: Friction Audit

Interview Product/Engineering leads to validate assumptions. Review lost deals, customer escalations, and integration pain points.

Week 3: Resource Leasing Test

Attempt to negotiate one small resource loan (e.g., "Can I borrow 1 engineer for 2 weeks to build a proof-of-concept?"). If this fails, the model is broken.

Week 4: Go/No-Go Recommendation

Present findings to leadership. Either: (1) Ratify the operating model with exec commitment to enforce it, OR (2) Recommend against the role because the org isn't ready.

The Ask

I'd appreciate 30 minutes with each of you in the next two weeks to pressure-test this hypothesis. Come prepared to poke holes in the model. Tell me what won't work. That's far more valuable than polite agreement.

If you're game, let's schedule time. If you're skeptical, even better—I need to know why before committing to an operating model that might fail.
