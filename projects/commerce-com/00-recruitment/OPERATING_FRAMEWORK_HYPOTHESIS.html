Operating Framework Hypothesis

Director of Strategic Solutions • Commerce.com • Hypothesis v2.1 CFO Validated

Executive Summary

The Role in Plain Language: I lead a small team that solves Commerce.com's most complex customer migration and integration problems. When a client needs to move from one commerce platform to another, or when we need to figure out how to make something work that's never been done before—that's what we tackle. The goal is to solve it once in a way that helps the next hundred customers.

We Build Jigs: Think of this like manufacturing. Platform teams (BigCommerce, Feedonomics, Makeswift) build the raw materials and tools. When a complex customer need comes in—like migrating 8,000 WooCommerce stores—we build the jig: the specialized fixture that holds everything together and makes the process repeatable. Once the jig is proven, we hand it to the production line (Core Product teams) and move on to the next complex problem.

What This Means for Commerce.com: Right now, when customers need migrations or integrations our standard platform doesn't offer, Solutions Architects build one-off "jigs" that stay on their laptops and never scale. This role industrializes the jig-making process: build it once with proper tooling, validate unit economics with real customers, document the playbook, then transfer it to Core Platform as a repeatable capability.

The Strategic Thesis: Commerce.com's acquisition of BigCommerce, Feedonomics, and Makeswift created powerful platform capabilities—but no one owns the "connective tissue" that turns these bricks into sellable houses. Sales sells the vision of unified commerce, but customers experience integration friction because vertical product teams optimize their own platforms, not cross-platform solutions.

The Operating Model: This role is a formal "Integrator" (Galbraith's term) that sits between Product and Engineering, forcing cross-platform coordination without re-organizing the company. It operates through Tiger Teams—temporary, small teams (3-5 people) that build integrated market solutions, validate them with real customers, then transfer ownership to Core Platform teams and disband.

Instance 001: WooCommerce Migration. WP Engine has identified 8,000 mid-market WooCommerce stores that need a migration path to BigCommerce. This is the validation instance for the operating model—build the migration capability with a 3-person team, prove unit economics with 10 pilot customers, then graduate it to Core Product.

This is Not a Final Operating Manual. This is a hypothesis-driven framework grounded in research from Galbraith, Tushman, and Team Topologies, validated against Fortune 500 post-M&A precedents. Use this document to critique my assumptions. Tell me where the theory doesn't apply to your reality. That conversation is the interview.

0. Theoretical Foundation

This operating model is not invented from scratch. It synthesizes three established frameworks validated across Fortune 500 organizations and post-M&A integrations:

Framework 1: Galbraith's Star Model (The Integrator Role)

Core Principle: In multi-dimensional organizations (product lines × geography × customer segments), lateral coordination requires formal "Integrator" roles.

Applied to Commerce.com:

  • Commerce.com has three product dimensions: BigCommerce Core, Feedonomics, Makeswift
  • Market solutions (WooCommerce Migration) require cross-product integration
  • Without an Integrator role, coordination relies on informal networks (slow, unreliable, political)

Hypothesis: The Director of Strategic Solutions is the formal Integrator for cross-platform market solutions.

Reference: Galbraith, J. (2009). Designing Matrix Organizations That Actually Work. Jossey-Bass.

Framework 2: Tushman & O'Reilly (Ambidextrous Organization)

Core Principle: Organizations must simultaneously exploit existing capabilities (incremental improvement) and explore new opportunities (breakthrough innovation). A single structure cannot optimize for both.

Applied to Commerce.com:

  • Exploitation: Core Product teams (BC, Feedonomics, Makeswift) optimize their platforms incrementally
  • Exploration: Tiger Teams explore new market solutions (WooCommerce Migration Kit)
  • Requires structural separation with integration mechanisms to prevent exploration from being starved by exploitation priorities

Hypothesis: Tiger Teams provide the structural separation for exploration, while Resource Leasing provides the integration mechanism.

Reference: Tushman, M. & O'Reilly, C. (2016). Lead and Disrupt: How to Solve the Innovator's Dilemma. Stanford Business Books.

Framework 3: Team Topologies (Enabling Team Pattern)

Core Principle: Platform teams often lack capacity to integrate their own capabilities into customer-facing solutions. "Enabling Teams" are temporary teams that help platform teams deliver value, then dissolve.

Applied to Commerce.com:

  • Platform Teams: BigCommerce, Feedonomics, Makeswift (maintain infrastructure)
  • Enabling Team: Tiger Team (forces integration, builds connective tissue, then transfers ownership)
  • Enabling Teams are not permanent—they dissolve after enabling the capability

Hypothesis: Tiger Teams are Enabling Teams that force platform integration for specific market opportunities, then graduate the solution to Core Product.

Reference: Skelton, M. & Pais, M. (2019). Team Topologies: Organizing Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow. IT Revolution Press.

0.5 Industry Precedents

This model has been validated across similar organizations facing post-acquisition integration challenges:

Salesforce (Post-Tableau, Slack, MuleSoft Acquisitions)

Challenge: Separate product lines (CRM, Collaboration, Integration) with minimal customer-facing integration.

Solution: Created "Solutions Engineering" overlay teams chartered to build cross-product offerings (Customer 360 platform).

Mechanism: Solutions teams had resource leasing agreements with product lines, with success metrics tied to unified solution adoption.

Outcome: Customer 360 became a major growth driver, demonstrating value of integrated platform positioning.

Microsoft (Satya Nadella's "One Microsoft")

Challenge: Azure, Office 365, Dynamics operated as separate P&Ls with competing go-to-market strategies.

Solution: Created cross-cloud "Integrator" roles (e.g., Modern Work solutions that span Azure + Teams + SharePoint).

Mechanism: Integrators had authority to define cross-product interfaces, with Core Platform teams required to honor API contracts.

Outcome: Commercial cloud revenue exceeded $100B run rate, attributed to integrated solution positioning.

Adobe Creative Cloud

Challenge: Photoshop, Illustrator, InDesign developed independently with no workflow integration.

Solution: Created "Workflow Solutions" team that built cross-tool integrations (Creative Cloud Libraries, shared asset management).

Mechanism: Workflow team defined integration schemas; tool teams implemented connectors. Solutions team dissolved after integration became core platform feature.

Outcome: Subscription model success driven by integrated creative suite value proposition.

Vista Equity Portfolio Companies (SaaS Roll-ups)

Challenge: 80+ acquired SaaS companies with overlapping capabilities and customer confusion.

Solution: Centralized "Solutions Architecture" function with resource leasing model. Solutions Architects had budget authority to "lease" engineering capacity from portfolio companies.

Mechanism: P&L attribution for participating companies, with revenue credited based on engineering contribution (measured in hours × hourly rate).

Outcome: 20%+ EBITDA margin improvements attributed to integrated solution positioning and reduced customer acquisition costs.

Key Pattern Across Precedents

All successful implementations share three characteristics:

  1. Formal Integrator Role: Not reliant on informal networks or "collaborative culture"
  2. Resource Leasing Mechanism: Integrator has mechanism to secure capacity from vertical teams
  3. Temporary Teams: Integration teams dissolve after capability is transferred to Core Platform

1. The "Broken State" Assumptions

Based on Commerce.com's structure as a composite of three acquired companies (BigCommerce, Feedonomics, Makeswift), I make the following assumptions about current organizational friction:

Assumption A1: Misaligned Roadmap Incentives
BigCommerce, Feedonomics, and Makeswift engineering teams currently have no shared incentive structure to align roadmaps.
Validation: Interview each VP of Engineering. Ask "What percentage of your roadmap is driven by cross-product integration needs vs. internal product roadmap?" If answer is <10%, assumption confirmed.
Assumption A2: The "Last Mile" Problem
Sales sells the "vision" of unified commerce (BC + Feedonomics + Makeswift working seamlessly), but customers experience integration friction because no one owns the "connective tissue."
Validation: Review lost deal post-mortems. Search for phrases like "integration complexity," "implementation timeline," or "requires custom development." If present in >30% of losses, assumption confirmed.
Assumption A3: Sales-Before-Feasibility Pattern
Sales commits to customer requirements (e.g., "We can migrate your WooCommerce store in 2 weeks") before Engineering has validated technical feasibility, creating delivery debt.
Validation: Ask Sales Engineering: "How often do you discover a technical blocker after the deal is signed?" If answer is "frequently" or "sometimes," assumption confirmed.
Assumption A4: No "Solution Product Manager" Archetype
Product Managers are organized vertically (PM for BC Core, PM for Feedonomics, PM for Makeswift). No one owns horizontal "market solutions" that span all three platforms.
Validation: Review org chart. Ask Vipul (CPO): "Who owns the WooCommerce migration opportunity as a product?" If answer is "no one" or "that's a partnership/GTM initiative," assumption confirmed.
Assumption A5: Resource Allocation via "Loudest Voice"
Engineering resources are allocated based on who escalates the loudest to the CEO, not based on strategic ROI or validated market demand.
Validation: Ask Marcus (SVP Eng): "How do you prioritize competing requests for engineering capacity?" If answer involves "executive escalation" or "political negotiation" more than "business case scoring," assumption confirmed.
Critical Test
If 3 or more of these assumptions validate as TRUE, the organization has a systemic execution problem that a traditional PM or VP cannot solve. This confirms the need for a "Context Conduit" role grounded in Galbraith's Integrator model.

2. L1 Strategic Workflow (Hypothesis)

The Concept: "The Mandate Lifecycle"

I hypothesize that Commerce.com lacks a structured process for moving from "strategic idea" to "shipped product" when that product requires cross-stack coordination. This is consistent with research on post-M&A organizations where vertical P&Ls resist cross-functional initiatives.

The Hypothesis: We need a lightweight, brutal stage-gate process that forces early validation and prevents "zombie projects."

Process Hypothesis: The Six-Stage Model

Stage 0: Strategic Signal (Week 1-2)
The "Rough Draft"
Input: Market signal (e.g., WPEngine CEO says "8,000 WooCommerce stores need migration path")
Activity: Director drafts 1-page hypothesis (Problem, Opportunity, Revenue Model, Risk)
Output: Executive Sponsor (CEO/CPO) approves exploration or kills immediately
Gate Criteria: "Is this worth 2 weeks of investigation?" YES → Proceed. NO → Kill.
Stage 1: Reality Check (Week 3-4)
Technical Feasibility Validation
Input: Approved hypothesis
Activity: Director + 1 Solutions Architect manually test the integration (e.g., migrate 1 WooCommerce store by hand)
Output: "Gap Analysis Report" documenting technical blockers and effort estimates
Gate Criteria: "Are there insurmountable technical barriers?" NO → Proceed. YES → Kill or Pivot.
Stage 2: Resource Trade (Week 5-6)
Executive Commitment
Input: Validated technical feasibility + revenue model
Activity: Director negotiates "resource leases" with functional VPs (e.g., "I need 2 Catalyst engineers for 12 weeks")
Output: Signed Resource Lease Agreements with P&L attribution terms
Gate Criteria: "Can I secure the people I need?" YES → Proceed. NO → Escalate to CEO or Kill.
Stage 3: Build (Week 7-18)
Tiger Team Execution
Input: Leased engineering resources + Gap Analysis requirements
Activity: Build the "Whole Product" (Code + Data Schema + SI Playbook + Demo)
Output: Functional prototype ready for real customer deployment
Gate Criteria: "Can we deploy this to a customer without it breaking?" YES → Proceed. NO → De-scope and retry.
Stage 4: First 10 Alpha (Week 19-26)
Customer Validation
Input: Functional prototype
Activity: Director acts as "Shadow SE" for first 10 customer deployments
Output: 10 reference customers, NPS score, validated unit economics
Gate Criteria: "TTFV <3 weeks? NPS >40? Unit economics profitable?" ALL YES → Proceed to Scale. ANY NO → Iterate or Kill.
Stage 5: Scale or Kill (Week 27+)
GTM Handoff or Termination
Input: Validated customer success metrics
Activity (if scaling): Transfer codebase to Core Engineering, transfer playbook to Sales, disband Tiger Team
Activity (if killing): Document learnings, archive code, reallocate resources
Outcome: Solution becomes a "Platform Brick" owned by Core Product, or initiative is terminated
Validation Question for Leadership

Does a process like this exist today? If not, do you have the political will to enforce hard stage gates? Research shows that without executive enforcement, exploration initiatives get starved by exploitation priorities (Tushman's finding).

3. L2 Tactical Workflow (Hypothesis)

The Concept: "The Integration Engine"

Even with executive buy-in, the tactical challenge is: How do you ship across silos without re-orging the company?

I hypothesize three mechanisms are required, grounded in Team Topologies research:

Mechanism 1: The "Guest Engineer" Model

Problem: You can't hire a standing "Tiger Team." Budget/headcount don't allow it.

Hypothesis: Functional VPs will temporarily "lease" engineers if they receive P&L attribution credit for the solution's revenue.

Research Basis: Vista Equity's internal market model demonstrated that P&L attribution creates voluntary resource sharing without re-org.

Test: Pitch Anthony Leach (VP Catalyst): "Loan me 1 React engineer for 12 weeks. I'll credit Catalyst with 20% of the WooCommerce migration ARR in internal reporting. Deal?"

Validation: If he agrees, model works. If he refuses, you either need CEO mandate or the model is broken.

Mechanism 2: The "Unblocker Sync" Ceremony

Problem: Status meetings are useless. You need decision-making meetings.

Hypothesis: A weekly 30-minute sync with one rule—"No status reports, only blockers and decisions"—can coordinate 3-4 distributed teams.

Research Basis: Team Topologies emphasizes "interaction modes" over org charts. Enabling Teams need explicit decision-making ceremonies.

Format:

  • Agenda Item 1 (10 min): What's blocked? (List only, no explanations)
  • Agenda Item 2 (15 min): Resolve top 2 blockers (Director makes the call, no consensus seeking)
  • Agenda Item 3 (5 min): Interface Contract status (Green/Red on each integration point)

Validation: Run this for 4 weeks. If blockers get resolved same-day, it works. If blockers linger for 2+ weeks, ceremony is broken.

Mechanism 3: The "Interface Contract" Artifact

Problem: "Who builds what" arguments kill velocity.

Hypothesis: If the Director owns the Interface Definition (the contract), and Platform teams own the Implementation (the code), arguments disappear.

Research Basis: Conway's Law states "Organizations design systems that mirror their communication structure." The Interface Contract explicitly defines the communication boundary.

Example:

  • Director defines: "I need an endpoint that accepts WooCommerce Product JSON and returns a BigCommerce Job ID."
  • Feedonomics builds: The transformation logic (their choice: Python, Node, whatever).
  • Contract: As long as the input/output schema matches, Director doesn't care how it's built.

Validation: If Feedonomics team pushes back with "that's not how we do things," you need executive air cover. Test this in Week 1.

3.5 Known Failure Modes (Anti-Patterns)

Research on Integrator roles and Enabling Teams has identified four failure modes that will kill this model if not mitigated:

Anti-Pattern 1: The "Permanent Tiger Team" Trap

Description: Tiger Teams never disband. They become another vertical silo competing for resources.

Research Source: Tushman & O'Reilly warn that exploration units must be temporary or they ossify into exploitation units.

Manifestation: Tiger Team starts owning "maintenance" of the WooCommerce Migration Kit instead of transferring it to Core Platform.

Mitigation: Hard 6-month dissolution rule. After Stage 5, Tiger Team must disband and solution ownership must transfer to Core Product. Director moves to Instance 002.

Anti-Pattern 2: The "Shadow IT" Problem

Description: Tiger Teams build infrastructure that Platform teams should own, creating technical debt and fragmentation.

Research Source: Galbraith's warning about Integrators overstepping into functional domain expertise.

Manifestation: Tiger Team builds its own deployment pipeline instead of using Platform DevOps infrastructure.

Mitigation: Interface Definition ownership rule. Director owns "what" (the contract), Platform owns "how" (the implementation). Code transfers to Platform post-launch.

Anti-Pattern 3: The "Resource Hostage" Risk

Description: Functional VPs refuse to lease resources, model collapses into political warfare.

Research Source: Matrix organization failures (Bartlett & Ghoshal, 1990) show that without executive enforcement, dual reporting structures fail.

Manifestation: VP Catalyst verbally agrees to lease engineer, then recalls them after 2 weeks for "urgent" roadmap work.

Mitigation: CEO must enforce Resource Lease Agreements. Revocation only permitted for P0 production incidents. If VPs systematically violate leases, model requires CEO mandate or it will fail.

Anti-Pattern 4: The "Success Tax"

Description: Tiger Team succeeds with First 10, Sales immediately over-sells before Platform can scale, solution collapses under demand.

Research Source: Marty Cagan's EMPOWERED warns about sales selling "roadmap futures" before product is production-ready.

Manifestation: First 10 WooCommerce migrations succeed. Sales signs 100 deals in Q2. Implementation team can't deliver. Customer churn ensues.

Mitigation: Hard gate between First 10 (Alpha) and Scale. Director has veto authority over deal acceptance during Pilot phase. Sales cannot sell solution until exit criteria met (Section 2, Stage 4).

Critical Question: Will Vipul's PM team, Marcus's engineering leads, and Jordan's product org understand the boundary between this role and theirs?

Test: Show org chart with new title. Ask: "Who owns the WooCommerce Migration opportunity?" If answer is unclear, title fails.

External Market Perception

Critical Question: When partners (WPEngine), customers, or agencies see this title on LinkedIn/email signatures, what will they assume?

Risk: If title suggests "pre-sales SE," inbound requests will be qualification calls instead of strategic partnership discussions.

Avoidance of Role Collision

Existing Roles to Avoid Confusing:

  • VP Product (Core): Vipul and his team own BC/Feedonomics/Makeswift platform roadmaps
  • Solutions Engineering: Pre-sales technical demos and proof-of-concepts
  • Solutions Architects (SI Partners): External agency implementation resources
  • Product Marketing: GTM positioning and messaging

The title must signal: "I orchestrate platform capabilities into sellable market solutions, but I don't own platform roadmaps or pre-sales engineering."
