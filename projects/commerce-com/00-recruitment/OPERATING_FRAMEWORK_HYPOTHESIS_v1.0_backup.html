Operating Framework Hypothesis

Director of Strategic Solutions • Commerce.com • Hypothesis v1.0 Draft for Validation

Document Purpose

This is not a final operating manual. This is a hypothesis-driven framework designed to:

  • Surface my assumptions about Commerce.com's current organizational dysfunction
  • Propose a testable operating model for cross-stack solution delivery
  • Outline how I will validate these assumptions and build the real manual in the first 30 days

Use this document to: Critique my assumptions. Tell me where I'm wrong. That conversation is the interview.

1. The "Broken State" Assumptions

Based on Commerce.com's structure as a composite of three acquired companies (BigCommerce, Feedonomics, Makeswift), I make the following assumptions about current organizational friction:

Assumption A1: Misaligned Roadmap Incentives
BigCommerce, Feedonomics, and Makeswift engineering teams currently have no shared incentive structure to align roadmaps.
Validation: Interview each VP of Engineering. Ask "What percentage of your roadmap is driven by cross-product integration needs vs. internal product roadmap?" If answer is <10%, assumption confirmed.
Assumption A2: The "Last Mile" Problem
Sales sells the "vision" of unified commerce (BC + Feedonomics + Makeswift working seamlessly), but customers experience integration friction because no one owns the "connective tissue."
Validation: Review lost deal post-mortems. Search for phrases like "integration complexity," "implementation timeline," or "requires custom development." If present in >30% of losses, assumption confirmed.
Assumption A3: Sales-Before-Feasibility Pattern
Sales commits to customer requirements (e.g., "We can migrate your WooCommerce store in 2 weeks") before Engineering has validated technical feasibility, creating delivery debt.
Validation: Ask Sales Engineering: "How often do you discover a technical blocker after the deal is signed?" If answer is "frequently" or "sometimes," assumption confirmed.
Assumption A4: No "Solution Product Manager" Archetype
Product Managers are organized vertically (PM for BC Core, PM for Feedonomics, PM for Makeswift). No one owns horizontal "market solutions" that span all three platforms.
Validation: Review org chart. Ask Vipul (CPO): "Who owns the WooCommerce migration opportunity as a product?" If answer is "no one" or "that's a partnership/GTM initiative," assumption confirmed.
Assumption A5: Resource Allocation via "Loudest Voice"
Engineering resources are allocated based on who escalates the loudest to the CEO, not based on strategic ROI or validated market demand.
Validation: Ask Marcus (SVP Eng): "How do you prioritize competing requests for engineering capacity?" If answer involves "executive escalation" or "political negotiation" more than "business case scoring," assumption confirmed.
Critical Test
If 3 or more of these assumptions validate as TRUE, the organization has a systemic execution problem that a traditional PM or VP cannot solve. This confirms the need for a "Context Conduit" role.

2. L1 Strategic Workflow (Hypothesis)

The Concept: "The Mandate Lifecycle"

I hypothesize that Commerce.com lacks a structured process for moving from "strategic idea" to "shipped product" when that product requires cross-stack coordination. The current state likely involves:

  • Good ideas die in PowerPoint because no one owns execution
  • Engineering builds features that don't align with GTM needs
  • Resources get re-allocated mid-project when a P0 incident occurs

The Hypothesis: We need a lightweight, brutal stage-gate process that forces early validation and prevents "zombie projects."

Process Hypothesis: The Six-Stage Model

Stage 0: Strategic Signal (Week 1-2)
The "Rough Draft"
Input: Market signal (e.g., WPEngine CEO says "8,000 WooCommerce stores need migration path")
Activity: Director drafts 1-page hypothesis (Problem, Opportunity, Revenue Model, Risk)
Output: Executive Sponsor (CEO/CPO) approves exploration or kills immediately
Gate Criteria: "Is this worth 2 weeks of investigation?" YES → Proceed. NO → Kill.
Stage 1: Reality Check (Week 3-4)
Technical Feasibility Validation
Input: Approved hypothesis
Activity: Director + 1 Solutions Architect manually test the integration (e.g., migrate 1 WooCommerce store by hand)
Output: "Gap Analysis Report" documenting technical blockers and effort estimates
Gate Criteria: "Are there insurmountable technical barriers?" NO → Proceed. YES → Kill or Pivot.
Stage 2: Resource Trade (Week 5-6)
Executive Commitment
Input: Validated technical feasibility + revenue model
Activity: Director negotiates "resource leases" with functional VPs (e.g., "I need 2 Catalyst engineers for 12 weeks")
Output: Signed Resource Lease Agreements with P&L attribution terms
Gate Criteria: "Can I secure the people I need?" YES → Proceed. NO → Escalate to CEO or Kill.
Stage 3: Build (Week 7-18)
Tiger Team Execution
Input: Leased engineering resources + Gap Analysis requirements
Activity: Build the "Whole Product" (Code + Data Schema + SI Playbook + Demo)
Output: Functional prototype ready for real customer deployment
Gate Criteria: "Can we deploy this to a customer without it breaking?" YES → Proceed. NO → De-scope and retry.
Stage 4: First 10 Alpha (Week 19-26)
Customer Validation
Input: Functional prototype
Activity: Director acts as "Shadow SE" for first 10 customer deployments
Output: 10 reference customers, NPS score, validated unit economics
Gate Criteria: "TTFV <3 weeks? NPS >40? Unit economics profitable?" ALL YES → Proceed to Scale. ANY NO → Iterate or Kill.
Stage 5: Scale or Kill (Week 27+)
GTM Handoff or Termination
Input: Validated customer success metrics
Activity (if scaling): Transfer codebase to Core Engineering, transfer playbook to Sales, disband Tiger Team
Activity (if killing): Document learnings, archive code, reallocate resources
Outcome: Solution becomes a "Platform Brick" owned by Core Product, or initiative is terminated
Validation Question for Leadership

Does a process like this exist today? If not, do you have the political will to enforce hard stage gates? If the answer is "we can't kill projects once started," this model won't work.

3. L2 Tactical Workflow (Hypothesis)

The Concept: "The Integration Engine"

Even with executive buy-in, the tactical challenge is: How do you ship across silos without re-orging the company?

I hypothesize three mechanisms are required:

Mechanism 1: The "Guest Engineer" Model

Problem: You can't hire a standing "Tiger Team." Budget/headcount don't allow it.

Hypothesis: Functional VPs will temporarily "lease" engineers if they receive P&L attribution credit for the solution's revenue.

Test: Pitch Anthony Leach (VP Catalyst): "Loan me 1 React engineer for 12 weeks. I'll credit Catalyst with 20% of the WooCommerce migration ARR in internal reporting. Deal?"

Validation: If he agrees, model works. If he refuses, you either need CEO mandate or the model is broken.

Mechanism 2: The "Unblocker Sync" Ceremony

Problem: Status meetings are useless. You need decision-making meetings.

Hypothesis: A weekly 30-minute sync with one rule—"No status reports, only blockers and decisions"—can coordinate 3-4 distributed teams.

Format:

  • Agenda Item 1 (10 min): What's blocked? (List only, no explanations)
  • Agenda Item 2 (15 min): Resolve top 2 blockers (Director makes the call, no consensus seeking)
  • Agenda Item 3 (5 min): Interface Contract status (Green/Red on each integration point)

Validation: Run this for 4 weeks. If blockers get resolved same-day, it works. If blockers linger for 2+ weeks, ceremony is broken.

Mechanism 3: The "Interface Contract" Artifact

Problem: "Who builds what" arguments kill velocity.

Hypothesis: If the Director owns the Interface Definition (the contract), and Platform teams own the Implementation (the code), arguments disappear.

Example:

  • Director defines: "I need an endpoint that accepts WooCommerce Product JSON and returns a BigCommerce Job ID."
  • Feedonomics builds: The transformation logic (their choice: Python, Node, whatever).
  • Contract: As long as the input/output schema matches, Director doesn't care how it's built.

Validation: If Feedonomics team pushes back with "that's not how we do things," you need executive air cover. Test this in Week 1.

4. The Day 1 to Day 30 Audit Plan

If hired, I will not impose this framework on Day 1. I will validate the assumptions and build the real manual based on ground truth.

Week 1

Objective: Validate Assumptions A1-A5 from Section 1.

Activities:

  • Mon-Tue: 1:1 interviews with Marcus (SVP Eng), Anthony (Catalyst), Feedonomics Lead, Makeswift Lead
  • Wed-Thu: 1:1 interviews with Vipul (CPO), Jordan (VP Product), Sales Engineering Lead
  • Fri: Review lost deal post-mortems and customer implementation escalations

Deliverable: "Friction Audit Report" documenting which assumptions validated and which didn't.

Week 2
The Pilot Process Test

Objective: Run one small decision through the proposed L1 Workflow (Section 2) to see where it breaks.

Test Case: WooCommerce Migration Feasibility (Stage 0 → Stage 1)

  • Mon: Draft 1-page hypothesis: "We can capture 5% of WooCommerce migrations"
  • Tue: Present to Travis (CEO) and Vipul (CPO) for Stage 0 approval
  • Wed-Fri: If approved, execute Stage 1 (manual migration of 1 test store with 1 Solutions Architect)

Observation Points:

  • Did Travis/Vipul approve or defer? (Tests executive decisiveness)
  • Could I get a Solutions Architect for 3 days? (Tests resource flexibility)
  • Did the manual migration surface technical blockers? (Tests feasibility assumptions)

Deliverable: "Process Test Report" documenting what worked and what broke.

Week 3
The Integration Engine Test

Objective: Test the L2 Tactical Workflow (Section 3) mechanisms.

Test 1: Guest Engineer Negotiation

  • Pitch Anthony Leach for 1 engineer, 2-week loan, with P&L attribution offer
  • If he agrees: Mechanism validated
  • If he refuses: Document why (budget constraints? political resistance? lack of exec mandate?)

Test 2: Unblocker Sync Ceremony

  • Run one "Unblocker Sync" with Catalyst + Feedonomics + Makeswift leads
  • Force the "no status, only decisions" rule
  • Observe: Do decisions get made in the meeting, or do they say "we need to take this offline"?

Test 3: Interface Contract

  • Draft a simple Interface Contract (e.g., "WooCommerce Product → BC Product transformation spec")
  • Present to Feedonomics: "Can you build to this spec?"
  • Observe: Do they accept the contract, or do they push back with "we need to redesign the spec"?

Deliverable: "Integration Engine Report" with green/red status on each mechanism.

Week 4
The Ratification

Objective: Present the validated Operating Framework to executive leadership.

Format: 30-minute presentation to CEO, CPO, SVP Eng

Agenda:

  • Slide 1-2: Friction Audit findings (which assumptions validated)
  • Slide 3-4: Proposed L1 Workflow (refined based on Week 2 test)
  • Slide 5-6: Proposed L2 Workflow (refined based on Week 3 tests)
  • Slide 7: "What Needs to Be True" for this to work (e.g., "You must enforce stage gates," "VPs must honor resource leases")
  • Slide 8: Recommendation: Approve Operating Framework v2.0 or Reject

Deliverable: Final Operating Framework v2.0 document based on validated ground truth.

Success Criteria for Day 30

By end of Week 4, I will have either: (1) A ratified Operating Framework that the exec team commits to enforcing, OR (2) Evidence that the org cannot support this operating model, in which case I recommend against the role.

5. What Needs to Be True

This operating model requires specific organizational capabilities. If these are not present, the model will fail:

Requirement Why It Matters Validation Test
Executive Decisiveness Stage gates only work if CEO/CPO will actually kill projects Ask: "Show me a project you killed in the last 6 months." If none, decisiveness is lacking.
VP Collaboration Willingness Resource leasing requires VPs to prioritize company goals over department empire-building Test: Attempt one resource lease negotiation. If VP refuses without exec mandate, collaboration is low.
Customer Access First 10 validation requires real customers, not test environments Ask Sales: "Can you get me 10 WooCommerce merchants willing to pilot?" If answer is "maybe" or "that's hard," access is constrained.
Technical Debt Tolerance Solutions require "duct tape" middleware that may not be production-grade initially Ask Engineering: "Can we ship a 'good enough' solution that we refactor later?" If answer is "everything must be perfect," velocity will suffer.
Sales Discipline Sales must not sell unvalidated solutions during Pilot phase Test: Tell Sales "You cannot sell WooCommerce migration until 10 pilots complete." If they agree, discipline exists. If they push back, it doesn't.
Kill Criteria
If 3 or more of these requirements fail validation during the 30-day audit, I will recommend against implementing this operating model. The organization is not ready, and forcing the model will cause political damage without delivering results.

6. Open Questions for Leadership

These are the questions I need answered during the interview process or in Week 1:

  1. Org Structure Clarity: Who reports to Vipul (CPO)? What's the actual PM structure across BC/Feedonomics/Makeswift?
  2. Current Decision-Making Process: How do cross-platform features get prioritized today? Is there a formal process or is it ad-hoc?
  3. Resource Allocation Reality: If I need 2 engineers from Anthony's Catalyst team for 12 weeks, who do I negotiate with—Anthony directly, Marcus, or Vipul?
  4. PMO Existence: Is there a Project Management Office? If not, who coordinates cross-functional execution today?
  5. Failed Initiative History: Show me a strategic initiative from the last 2 years that failed. What killed it? (This reveals the real constraints.)
  6. WPEngine Partnership Status: Is the WPEngine partnership real or speculative? Do we have a signed agreement or just exploratory conversations?
  7. Budget Authority: Does this role have budget authority to pay for external contractors (e.g., offshore dev shop for middleware build)?
