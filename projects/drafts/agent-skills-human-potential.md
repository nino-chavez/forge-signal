# The Graveyard Isn't Full of Bad Ideas

How many ideas die as the dream as a person wakes up?

I keep thinking about that question. Not in the abstract, productivity-guru sense of "capture your morning thoughts." More like: how much potential goes unrealized simply because the gap between *having* an idea and *executing* it is so wide that most people never make it across?

---

## The Friction Tax

I've watched it happen. A friend with a legitimate product concept spends three months learning React just to get a landing page up. By month four, the enthusiasm is gone. The idea joins the graveyard.

A colleague sketches out an internal tool that would save her team twenty hours a week. But she's not a developer, and IT has a six-month backlog. The sketch stays in her notebook.

This is the friction tax. It's not that the ideas were bad. It's that the technical distance between "I see it" and "it exists" was too far to walk.

---

## What Vercel Just Did

Vercel released something called Agent Skills this week—essentially a package manager for AI coding agents. The headline feature: ten years of React and Next.js optimization rules, packaged into installable modules that agents can apply automatically.

On the surface, it sounds like developer tooling. Another framework. Another abstraction.

But I think it's something else.

What they've done is package *engineering judgment*. The kind of intuition that takes years to develop—knowing when to memoize, how to structure for accessibility, what patterns lead to maintainable code—and made it installable. Transferable. Available to anyone with an idea and an AI assistant.

---

## The Shift I'm Watching

Here's where my thinking has been evolving.

The old model was: You have an idea. You learn to code (or hire someone who can). You spend months on implementation details. Maybe you ship. Probably you don't.

The emerging model is different: You have an idea. You describe it to an agent. The agent applies accumulated engineering wisdom—security patterns, performance optimizations, deployment standards—and produces something that actually works.

The human stays in the vision layer. The agent handles the deterministic execution.

I used to think this was about making developers faster. Now I'm not sure that's the interesting part. The interesting part might be what happens when non-developers can finally cross the gap.

---

## The Uncomfortable Question

Some people look at this and see an attack on jobs. I understand the instinct. If agents can apply ten years of expertise automatically, what happens to the people who spent ten years acquiring it?

But I keep coming back to a different frame.

What if the constraint was never talent? What if it was access?

There are people with genuine product instincts who will never ship because they can't clear the technical bar. There are domain experts who see exactly what software should exist for their field but can't build it. There are ideas sitting in notebooks, on whiteboards, in voice memos—fully formed visions with no path to reality.

The friction tax isn't evenly distributed. It falls hardest on people who aren't already inside the technical circle.

---

## What Changes

If this plays out the way I think it might, we're looking at something like what happened with literacy. For most of human history, if you had an idea worth sharing, you needed access to scribes, then to printing presses, then to publishers. Each layer removed some friction. Each removal expanded who could participate.

We might be at another one of those transitions. Not for sharing ideas—we solved that with the internet—but for *building* them. For turning vision into artifact.

The person with the idea becomes the driver again. The technical implementation becomes the service layer.

---

## Where I'm Still Uncertain

I don't want to oversell this. Agent Skills is a developer tool, and today it mostly helps developers work faster. The "anyone can build" future is still more promise than reality.

And there's a question I haven't resolved: What gets lost when expertise becomes invisible? When optimization rules are packaged and applied automatically, do we lose the understanding of *why* they matter? Does the craft dimension of software development erode?

Maybe. I'm watching for it.

But I keep returning to that graveyard of unfinished projects. The ideas that died not because they were bad, but because the execution gap was too wide.

If we can narrow that gap—even partially—we unlock something. Not replacement of human capability. Expansion of it.

That's the frame I'm holding, anyway. Ask me again in six months.
